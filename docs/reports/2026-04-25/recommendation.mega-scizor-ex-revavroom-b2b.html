Game Plan

This deck is a steel-focused pressure shell built around Mega Scizor ex plus a Revavroom package, with high consistency support and layered utility tools. The most stable backbone is clear from sample metrics: Mega Scizor ex, Revavroom, Scyther, Poké Ball, and Professor's Research are all avg_count 2.0 with presence_rate 1.0, while Copycat and Metal Core Barrier are frequent support pieces (avg_count 1.6667 and 1.3333, both presence_rate 1.0). In ranked play, the practical plan is to establish early board with Scyther/Varoom lines, convert quickly into Mega Scizor ex pressure, and use trainer utility to preserve tempo through trades.

Game Plan Stages

Open

Mega Scizor ex

Prioritize opening hands with at least one base from each functional line (Scyther and/or Varoom) plus one consistency card (Poké Ball or Professor's Research). Sequence search/draw first to secure evolution continuity before spending singleton disruption cards. If both attacker lines are available, establish the line with the fastest immediate pressure while keeping the other on bench as follow-up so you can absorb early disruption without losing tempo.

Mid

Poké Ball

Transition into steady pressure by promoting Mega Scizor ex once set up, while using Revavroom line as a backup or parallel threat to avoid single-point failure. Use Copycat and Professor's Research to keep evolution and tool/support flow active each turn. Deploy Metal Core Barrier and Training Area in turns where they convert into extra survivability or attack continuity, not merely for incremental value. Because current archetype win rate is below 50% (48.22), favor low-variance sequencing and guaranteed attacks over speculative lines.

Close

Professor's Research

In late game, commit resources to whichever active line has the shortest guaranteed finish route, and preserve one interaction card (for example Cyrus/support utility) for the decisive turn. If Mega Scizor ex is answered, pivot immediately into prepared Revavroom pressure instead of rebuilding from basics. Close by maximizing attack continuity and minimizing empty turns; in this archetype profile, lost tempo is harder to recover than modest damage inefficiency.

Common Pitfalls

  • Overcommitting to one attacker line and failing to stage the second line, leading to collapse after one knockout/disruption turn.
  • Using singleton disruption/support cards too early instead of saving them for clear tempo swing turns.
  • Cutting core consistency cards despite their universal presence_rate 1.0 in sampled lists.
  • Treating category=None substitute candidates as equal to role-matched swaps; many imply archetype shifts rather than true side-grades.
  • Mis-timing protection/utility tools so they do not actually buy an extra attack turn.
